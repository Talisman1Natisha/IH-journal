About this project

This website distills a semester's worth of notes, doodles and reflections into a concise visual overview. For each film, you'll find artwork alongside brief essays exploring themes that resonated with our readings by Orwell, Confucius, Kimmerer, Fanon, Anzaldúa, de Pizan and Žižek. Romero (1989)

Private Privilege vs. The Common Good

A wealthy woman asks Archbishop Romero for a private baptism because she does not want to share the church with the country’s poor. Romero refuses, insisting that there are “no private baptisms anymore.” The conflict recalls George Orwell’s critique in The Road to Wigan Pier: middle‑class reformers support equality in theory but recoil from proximity to those who labor for them. By holding the baptism in the crowded nave, Romero insists that a just community is built when privileges, spiritual or economic, are shared, not hoarded.

Mandate of Heaven vs. Unjust Rule

At breakfast, the President‑Elect and a general discuss “law and order” while feasting on lavish food. When Romero pleads for information about a missing priest, they dismiss him with claims that development and jobs justify their regime. Confucius’s Analects argue that legitimate rule requires te (virtue) and ren (humaneness). Without benevolence and attention to individual suffering, material growth becomes mere tyranny, what Fanon might call colonial violence in a new form.

Reciprocity and Hoarding

Endless rows of coffee and sugarcane fields fill the screen while emaciated campesinos harvest for export. Robin Wall Kimmerer’s concept of the “Honorable Harvest” insists we take only what we need and leave enough for the community and the earth to heal. El Salvador’s elites exhibit a Windigo mindset, exporting wealth and leaving their neighbors hungry. Romero’s liberation theology echoes Indigenous wisdom: the land belongs to the community (or God), not to those with the largest bank accounts.

The Universal “We”

In his final sermon, broadcast on the radio, Romero turns to the soldiers and implores: “I beg you, I implore you, I order you… stop the repression!” Gloria Anzaldúa’s idea of a mestiza consciousness, an identity that embraces multiplicity, resonates here. By reminding the soldiers that they are killing their own sisters and brothers, Romero seeks to heal the borders erected between oppressor and oppressed. The common good demands we recognize a shared humanity beyond class or uniform.

Building the City of God

In Aguilares, soldiers convert a church into a barracks. Later Romero leads the people in reclaiming the sanctuary, adorning it with flowers and song. Christine de Pizan’s City of Ladies envisions an allegorical city built by Reason, Rectitude and Justice to protect those defamed by power. When Romero and the peasants cleanse their church and return to worship, they lay a brick in that moral city. The common good becomes a physical space where virtue, not violence, offers security.

Violence and the Fracture of the Common

Father Osuna is tortured by the army; guerillas kidnap and kill members of the elite. Frantz Fanon reminds us in The Wretched of the Earth that what the colonizer calls “peace” is often simply compliance under duress. Romero hopes for a non‑violent common good, but Fanon would argue that decolonization inevitably involves bloodshed. The cycle of violence reveals how far society has drifted from any shared moral order.

Radical Solidarity in Crisis

In perhaps the film’s most moving sequence, Romero enters a town occupied by soldiers to retrieve the Eucharist. One by one, the townspeople follow him, forming a human shield. Slavoj Žižek’s pandemic essays argue that true common good demands “war communism” in times of crisis, a radical solidarity that transcends individual survival. By risking their lives for the dignity of their community, these villagers embody the ethic Žižek describes.

Night of the Living Dead (1968)

Failure of the Collective

The central conflict between Ben and Harry Cooper echoes Confucius’s insistence on ritual propriety (li) and humaneness (ren). Ben boards windows, organizes defences and urges cooperation; Harry wants to retreat to the cellar with his family. The farmhouse becomes a microcosm of class struggle: the upstairs represents collective action, embodied by the Black working‑class protagonist; the cellar represents bourgeois hoarding. Without a shared moral order, the group descends into chaos.

The Outsider and the State

Ben survives the night only to be mistaken for a ghoul and shot by a white sheriff’s posse at dawn. Fanon’s analysis of the colonized subject is painfully literal here: the Black man is rendered invisible as a human and instead perceived as a monster. Anzaldúa’s border theory suggests Ben inhabits an in‑between space, neither accepted by the white family in the house nor fully assimilated into the zombie horde. His death underscores how the “common good” constructed by state authorities excludes those marked as other.

Crisis and Solidarity

As survivors huddle around radios and televisions, confused government broadcasts blame a Venusian probe for the outbreak. Žižek likens such crises to pandemics: without radical solidarity, we revert to barbarism. The characters fail to internalize that they are “all in the same boat,” turning on one another instead of the undead outside.

Gender and Voice

Women in the farmhouse are silenced or ignored. Barbara is catatonic; Judy is scolded to “just sit there”; Helen Cooper’s practicality is overruled by her husband. Christine de Pizan’s Book of the City of Ladies imagines a city where women’s voices construct the common good. The collapse of the farmhouse-city suggests that when women are barred from decision‑making, the social fabric tears as surely as boards splinter under ghoul hands.

The Burmese Harp (1956)

The Common Good & Philosophical Connections

Confucius teaches that true common good emerges when rites and benevolence are observed even toward the dead. Mizushima refuses to return home until he has buried the soldiers’ bodies, restoring the moral order of the universe. Gloria Anzaldúa would call him a nepantlera: one who walks between worlds (life and death, Japan and Burma) to heal a cultural wound. Kimmerer’s emphasis on reciprocity resonates. Mizushima gives his labor freely to the fallen who can offer nothing material in return. Fanon argued that colonial violence demands a violent response, yet this film imagines a post‑violence act of restitution. Žižek suggests catastrophe forces us to see what matters; for Mizushima, only compassion and burial endure when empire falls.

Battle of Algiers (1966)

The Fractured Common Good

Colonel Mathieu warns the French public: if Algeria remains part of France, you must accept the “consequences.” George Orwell reminds us that comforts enjoyed by the metropole are built on hidden violence, in this case, torture and occupation. The common good of the colonizer requires the suffering of the colonized. Fanon argues that such a system cannot be reformed; it must be overthrown.

Who Defines “Order”?

A propaganda voiceover claims the FLN is “a minority which rules through terror and violence”; the response is to isolate and destroy them. Confucius says a ruler should lead by virtue so that the people follow like grass bending before the wind. The French, ruling without benevolence, have forfeited their Mandate of Heaven. Žižek notes that states treat rebellion as disease, imposing curfews and suspending rights. In the name of the common good, torture is rationalized and due process forgotten.

Identity & Borders

Algerian women cut their hair and don Western dresses to slip through checkpoints and plant bombs. Anzaldúa’s Borderlands describes living between cultures. Here, women weaponize that liminality, becoming literal shapeshifters for the revolution. Christine de Pizan argued that women are builders of cities; in Algiers they build a new nation by destroying colonial architecture.

Land & Extraction

Tanks roll over narrow streets; checkpoints carve the city into zones. Kimmerer teaches that land is a relative, not a resource. Colonialism treats it as something to be extracted and policed. The Casbah fighters move organically through their environment; the French feel like an imposition. The common good for Algerians means returning the land to those who live in reciprocity with it.
